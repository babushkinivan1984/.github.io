A Letter from the Stomach to Dr. Singer

Today

Dear Dr. Singer, it's me, the stomach.

You probably don't recognize me. I used to be strong and inconspicuous, as I should be. My work was rhythmic and precise: receive, mix, send onward. I never caused any trouble. Ivan and I lived in perfect harmony.

Then everything changed.

First, an Alien took up residence inside me. It was large and hard. It interfered with my work, pressed against my walls, distorted my shape. I sent signals—a dull pain, a feeling of fullness—but for a long time, no one understood them.

Then you came, Dr. Singer. You found my intruder. You said it was evil, that its name was GIST, and that it could kill both me and Ivan. I was terrified. But you said there would be a battle, and that you would save us.

And then came the day of the battle.

I remember the cold of anesthesia, and then—the cutting pain. Not from the Alien, but from you. Your hands breached my fortress, my walls. You cut away a whole piece of me—the part where the Alien was lodged. You cut me right near the Entrance, the most important place where I connect to the Supreme Pathway. It was terrifying and painful. You sewed me back together with a new, unfamiliar seam. It pulls and stings.

I woke up in a new world, Dr. Singer. A world where I am disabled.

My room became smaller. Now, when Ivan puts food inside me, I fill up almost instantly, and I have nowhere to put it. I used to be a spacious hall; now I am a cramped closet. My muscles, which once worked like a harmonious orchestra, are now confused. Sometimes I push the food onward too quickly, and then Ivan's whole body weakens and breaks out in a sweat. It's my fault.

I hear Ivan say: "My stomach is disabled." And I know he is right.

But, Dr. Singer, I want to tell you one thing. I am not angry with you.

Because you were right about one thing: you killed the Alien. You cut it out. And when your colleagues put it under glass and examined it, it turned out it wasn't an evil GIST, but just a Cyst. A rough, large, but not fatal one.

You fought what you believed was a dragon, but you killed a shadow. And I, your ally, sustained the heaviest wounds in that battle.

So who am I now, Dr. Singer?

I am not a hero, because I didn't defeat a real enemy. I am not a victim, because you acted to save a life. I am a casualty of friendly fire.

My request to you, Dr. Singer, is not a medical one. It is a human one. Please, help Ivan understand. Tell him not to blame me. To not call me disabled. To help me.

I am not asking for my past back. I am asking for help to accept the present. Give us—give him—the rules to live by. Small portions. Quiet, soft food. Time. And patience.

I can no longer work as I did before. But I can still work. I am still a STOMACH. It's just that now, I am a stomach that survived the operation you performed. And I want Ivan and me to be a team again. Even if crippled, we are alive, thanks to you and yet because of you.

Sincerely,

Your former patient.
